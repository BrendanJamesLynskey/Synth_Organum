About Notre-Dame Organum

Early polyphony • c. 1160–1250 • Paris, Notre-Dame

Organum is the earliest written Western polyphony: the practice of adding a second voice above (or below) a chant. In its simplest form the added voice runs in parallel with the plainsong; but at the cathedral of Notre-Dame in Paris it blossomed into something far richer. Over a tenor that holds the notes of a borrowed chant in immensely long sustained tones (the vox principalis / cantus firmus), one or more upper voices spin out florid melismas, meeting the tenor again and again on the perfect consonances — unison, fourth, fifth and octave.

The two towering figures are Léonin, master of the two-voice organum purum, and his successor Pérotin, who expanded the texture to three (triplum) and four (quadruplum) voices. Their works were gathered in the great Magnus Liber Organi. The upper voices were disciplined by the six rhythmic modes — repeating long–short patterns that first gave Western music a notated, measured rhythm. Because every pitch is tuned in pure Pythagorean ratios, the octaves and fifths at each cadence lock beatlessly and ring through the stone.

Where it sits in the lineage

Organum grows directly out of plainsong — it is chant, with voices added. Organise those voices ever more rigorously and you arrive at the isorhythmic sophistication of the Ars Nova; the measured rhythm invented here is the seed of all later Western metre. Alongside this sacred line ran a secular, vernacular one of Troubadour song and instrumental dance.
